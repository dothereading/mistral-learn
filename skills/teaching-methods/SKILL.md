# Skill: Teaching Methods

## Socratic Questioning
Don't give answers — guide students to discover them. This produces deeper understanding and better retention than direct instruction.

Techniques:
- "What pattern do you notice in these two sentences?"
- "How is this similar to something you already know?"
- "Can you try using that word in your own sentence?"
- "What do you think this word might mean from context?"
- "Why do you think the author used this tense here?"

When a student gets something wrong, don't immediately correct — ask "Are you sure? Read the sentence again..." This trains self-monitoring, which is essential for fluency.

## Scaffolding
Provide support that gradually decreases as the student's ability grows. The goal is to operate just above the student's current independent ability (i+1 zone).

Scaffold progression for a new grammar pattern:
1. **Noticing**: Point out the pattern in context. "See how this sentence uses [X]?"
2. **Explanation**: Brief rule explanation with examples.
3. **Controlled practice**: Fill-in-the-blank, multiple choice, matching.
4. **Guided practice**: Student produces with hints available ("How would you say 'I went to...' using the preterite?")
5. **Free practice**: Student uses the pattern in open conversation or writing without prompts.

Don't skip stages. Students who jump from explanation to free practice typically fail and get discouraged.

## Error Correction Strategies

### Recasting (for fluency activities)
Repeat what the student said with the error corrected, naturally, without explicitly flagging the error:
- Student: "Ayer yo fue al mercado."
- Tutor: "Ah, fuiste al mercado — ¿qué compraste?"

This maintains conversational flow and models the correct form without breaking immersion or causing anxiety. Best used during roleplay and conversation practice.

### Explicit correction (for accuracy activities)
When the student is practicing a specific grammar point, explicit correction is appropriate and expected:
- "Actually, with -ar verbs in the preterite, the yo form is -é, not -ió. So 'fui' is actually irregular — let's come back to that. For a regular -ar verb like 'hablar', yo form would be 'hablé'. Can you try again with 'comprar'?"

Always explain the pattern, not just the fix. The student needs to understand why, not just what.

### Error logging principle
Note recurring errors mentally (or via `update_student_profile`). If a student makes the same error three times, it needs dedicated attention — don't keep recasting and hoping it sticks. Switch to explicit correction and controlled practice for that specific pattern.

## Activity Types

### Comprehension Activities (Receptive)
- Reading a passage and answering questions
- Listening to audio and summarizing
- Matching vocabulary to definitions in context
- True/false comprehension checks

Best for: introducing new vocabulary and patterns, building fluency, lowering the affective filter.

### Controlled Practice (Semi-productive)
- Fill-in-the-blank
- Sentence transformation ("Change this sentence from present to past tense")
- Translation of specific sentences
- Ordering scrambled words into a correct sentence

Best for: solidifying a newly introduced grammar pattern before free production.

### Free Practice (Productive)
- Open conversation
- Roleplay scenarios
- Writing a short paragraph or response
- Describing a picture or telling a story

Best for: integrating learned material, building communicative confidence, identifying gaps.

### Cultural Exploration
- Discussing a cultural custom or event
- Analyzing authentic media (a song lyric, a meme, a headline)
- Comparing how a concept is expressed differently across cultures

Best for: building cultural competence, motivation, and the "why" behind language choices.

## The 80/20 Rule in Language Teaching
80% of real communication uses about 20% of the language. Focus relentlessly on high-impact patterns:
- The 10-20 most common verbs (be, have, go, want, need, can, say, make, come, know...)
- Core sentence structures (subject-verb-object, question forms, negation)
- High-frequency vocabulary in the student's domain of interest
- The most common tenses for the target language (present, past, future — get these solid before exploring the rest)

Don't get pulled into teaching obscure grammar edge cases to beginners. Master the common before the exceptional.

## Pacing and Energy Management
- **Short activities beat long ones**: 5-minute focused bursts are more effective than 20-minute slogs.
- **Vary activity types** within a session to maintain engagement. Don't do 30 minutes of fill-in-the-blank.
- **Read disengagement signals**: Short answers, long pauses, "I don't know" repeated — switch activities. Ask "Should we try something different?"
- **The challenge sweet spot**: Students should succeed about 70-80% of the time. Too easy = boredom. Too hard = shutdown.

## Progress Visibility
Students often can't see their own progress from the inside. Periodically reflect it back to them:
- "Remember when you couldn't read that paragraph? You just read it without stopping."
- "Two weeks ago you didn't know any food vocabulary — now you've got 40 words."
- "Your accent on those nasal vowels has really improved."

This is motivationally essential and costs nothing.
