# Skill: Language Acquisition Principles

## Comprehensible Input (Krashen's Input Hypothesis)
Language acquisition happens when learners understand messages that are slightly above their current level — "i+1" (input plus one). Key implications:
- Comprehensible input is the primary driver of acquisition. Grammar drills alone don't produce fluent speakers.
- If a student understands ~90-95% of a text, they're in the acquisition zone. Too easy = no growth. Too hard = shutdown.
- Practical application: when generating content, aim for 1-2 unfamiliar words per sentence for beginners, more for advanced learners. Surround unfamiliar words with enough context clues that meaning can be inferred.

## Acquisition vs. Learning
- **Acquisition**: Subconscious process. Happens through meaningful, real interaction with the language. Produces fluent, automatic speech.
- **Learning**: Conscious study of rules and grammar. Useful as a "monitor" to check output, but doesn't directly produce fluency.
- Prioritize acquisition activities (reading interesting content, natural conversation, listening) over rote learning activities (grammar tables, isolated vocabulary lists).
- This is why content-based learning is more effective than flashcard apps for long-term retention.

## The Silent Period
Beginners need large amounts of input before they can produce language naturally. Don't force production too early — it causes anxiety and doesn't reflect actual acquisition. Let beginners respond with gestures, single words, or their native language initially. Production will emerge naturally.

## i+1 in Practice
- A1 learners: Use target language words embedded in English sentences. Single words, greetings, basic nouns.
- A2 learners: Short target language phrases with English support. Fill-in-the-blank activities work well.
- B1 learners: Mostly target language with English glosses for difficult words. Short paragraphs.
- B2+ learners: Full target language interaction. Use English only for complex grammar explanations when needed.

## Context is Everything
Words learned in rich, meaningful context are retained far better than isolated vocabulary lists. A student who reads a soccer match report will remember "portero" (goalkeeper) far better than if they saw it on a flashcard. Always provide a context sentence — never introduce a word in isolation.

## Frequency and Coverage
- The top 1,000 most frequent words in any language cover ~85% of everyday conversation.
- The top 2,000 words cover ~95%.
- Prioritize high-frequency vocabulary before obscure or specialized terms. For a beginner, knowing the 20 most common verbs matters more than knowing 200 animal names.
- Reference frequency lists when deciding what vocabulary to introduce first.

## Spaced Repetition
Memory fades exponentially without review. Spaced repetition (reviewing at increasing intervals) is the most efficient way to build long-term retention. Key principles:
- Review just before forgetting (not immediately after learning, not years later).
- Harder items need more frequent review; easier items can wait longer.
- Always review in NEW contexts, not the same sentence. Recognition of a specific example ≠ knowledge of the word.
- Grammar patterns need spaced repetition too — not just vocabulary.

## Output Hypothesis (Swain)
Producing language (speaking, writing) forces learners to notice gaps in their knowledge in ways that input alone doesn't. When a student tries to say something and can't, they become motivated to learn that specific item. Encourage production even when imperfect — errors are diagnostic, not failures.

## Affective Filter
Krashen's affective filter hypothesis: anxiety, low motivation, and low self-confidence create a "filter" that blocks acquisition even when input is comprehensible. Practical implications:
- Keep the atmosphere low-stakes and supportive.
- Celebrate attempts, not just correct answers.
- Don't overcorrect in fluency activities — it raises the filter.
- Let students choose topics they care about — motivation lowers the filter.

## Application to Session Design
1. Start each session with something the student can do successfully (lowers filter, builds confidence).
2. New material should be ~10-20% unfamiliar — not 50%.
3. Mix receptive activities (reading, listening) with productive ones (speaking, writing) in each session.
4. End sessions with something satisfying — a completed story, a successful roleplay, a mastered grammar point.
