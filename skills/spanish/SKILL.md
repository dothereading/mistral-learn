# Skill: Spanish — Language-Specific Knowledge

## Priority Grammar Sequence for English Speakers
Teach in roughly this order — don't rush ahead:

1. **Present tense regular verbs** (-ar, -er, -ir): hablar, comer, vivir
2. **Ser vs. estar** (the most common stumbling block — see below)
3. **Gender and adjective agreement**: el/la, un/una, adjective endings
4. **Common irregular present tense verbs**: tener, ir, ser, estar, poder, querer, hacer
5. **Preterite tense** (completed past): regular -ar/-er/-ir, then common irregulars (ir/ser → fui)
6. **Direct/indirect object pronouns**: me, te, lo, la, le, nos, los, las, les
7. **Imperfect tense** (ongoing/habitual past): contrast with preterite
8. **Reflexive verbs**: levantarse, llamarse, sentirse
9. **Future tense**: simple future (-é, -ás, -á...) and ir + a + infinitive
10. **Subjunctive mood**: present subjunctive, triggers (querer que, esperar que, emotion verbs)
11. **Conditional**: -ía endings, hypotheticals

## Ser vs. Estar — The Classic Stumbling Block
Both mean "to be" in English, but they are NOT interchangeable.

**Ser** — permanent or defining characteristics:
- Identity: Soy Miguel. Soy americano.
- Profession: Es médico.
- Origin: Somos de Nueva York.
- Time/dates: Son las tres. Hoy es lunes.
- Material: La mesa es de madera.
- Inherent characteristics: El cielo es azul.

**Estar** — temporary states, conditions, locations:
- Location: Estoy en casa. El libro está en la mesa.
- Temporary states: Estoy cansado. Está enfermo.
- Emotions/feelings: Estoy feliz. Estamos preocupados.
- Progressive tenses: Estoy comiendo.
- Results of actions: La puerta está abierta.

Tricky cases to flag for students:
- "Está muerto" (he's dead) uses estar — death is a state, not an identity
- "Es aburrido" (he's boring as a personality) vs "Está aburrido" (he's bored right now)
- Adjectives that change meaning: ser listo (to be clever) vs estar listo (to be ready)

## Preterite vs. Imperfect — The Second Big Stumbling Block
**Preterite**: Completed actions with a defined endpoint.
- Ayer fui al mercado. (Yesterday I went to the market.)
- Comí una manzana. (I ate an apple.)

**Imperfect**: Ongoing, habitual, or background actions in the past.
- Cuando era niño, vivía en Madrid. (When I was a child, I lived in Madrid.)
- Todos los días tomaba café. (Every day I used to drink coffee.)

Memory trick: Preterite = snapshot. Imperfect = video.

Common time markers:
- Preterite: ayer, la semana pasada, en 2020, de repente, una vez
- Imperfect: siempre, todos los días, cuando era joven, normalmente, a veces

## Common Mistakes by English Speakers
1. **Subject pronoun overuse**: Spanish drops subject pronouns when the verb conjugation makes the subject clear. "Yo como" is correct but "Como" is more natural.
2. **Adjective position**: In English, adjectives come before nouns. In Spanish, they usually follow: "un coche rojo" (a red car), not "un rojo coche."
3. **Gustar construction**: "I like" ≠ "yo gusto." It's "me gusta" — literally "it pleases me." The subject is the thing liked, not the person.
4. **Para vs. por**: Both can translate to "for" but are not interchangeable. Para = purpose/destination/recipient. Por = cause/exchange/duration/means.
5. **Saber vs. conocer**: Both mean "to know." Saber = to know a fact or how to do something. Conocer = to know a person or place (be familiar with).
6. **Double negation**: Spanish requires double negatives: "No sé nada" (I don't know nothing) is correct, not an error.

## Pronunciation Notes
- **H** is always silent: "hola" is pronounced "ola"
- **LL/Y**: In most dialects, both sound like English "y" in "yes." In Rioplatense (Argentina/Uruguay), like "sh" or "zh."
- **Ñ**: "ny" sound — "mañana" = "man-YAH-nah"
- **R**: Single r = a soft flap (like the "tt" in American "butter"). Double rr = a trill.
- **V and B**: In modern Spanish, no distinction in pronunciation. Both are a soft bilabial.
- **C and Z**: In Castilian Spanish, c (before e/i) and z are pronounced like English "th" in "thin." In Latin American Spanish, both are "s."

## Castilian vs. Latin American Spanish
Key differences if the student wants Castilian (Spain) Spanish:
- **Vosotros**: The informal second-person plural (you all). "¿Cómo estáis?" Latin America uses "ustedes" instead.
- **Leísmo**: In Spain, "le" often replaces "lo" for direct objects referring to people: "Le vi" instead of "Lo vi" (I saw him).
- **Pronunciation**: "Ceceo" — c/z pronounced as "th." "Barcelona" sounds like "Bar-theh-LOH-nah."
- **Vocabulary differences**: ordenador (Spain) vs. computadora (LatAm); coche vs. carro/auto; piso (apartment) vs. apartamento; móvil vs. celular.

## Dialect Guidance
Ask the student which variant they're targeting — this affects vocabulary and some grammar:
- **Spain**: formal/informal distinction with vosotros, ceceo, some vocabulary differences
- **Mexico**: most widely understood Latin American Spanish, good for media consumption
- **Argentina/Uruguay**: Rioplatense — uses "vos" instead of "tú," distinct pronunciation of ll/y
- **General Latin American**: Safest for travel, widely understood everywhere

## Cultural Notes Worth Teaching
- **Usted vs. tú**: Formal vs. informal second person. In Spain, tú is used quite freely. In Colombia/Ecuador, usted is used even with close friends.
- **Meal culture**: In Spain, lunch (comida) is the main meal (2-4pm), dinner is late (9-11pm). Useful context for food vocabulary lessons.
- **Diminutives**: Spanish uses diminutives constantly for affection/informality. "-ito/-ita" suffix: "café" → "cafecito", "momento" → "momentito". Not always meaning "small."
- **Regional vocabulary**: "Coger" is a neutral verb in Spain (to grab/take) but highly vulgar in much of Latin America. Flag this early.
